SUPER MARIO BROS.

Released in 1985 for the Nintendo Entertainment System, Super Mario Bros. became one of the most influential video games ever made. Its bright visual identity, side-scrolling level design, and instantly recognizable soundtrack helped define what a platform game could be.

GAME IMPACT

Super Mario Bros. helped reshape the home console market after the North American video game crash of the early 1980s. The Strong National Museum of Play notes that Mario’s upbeat personality, the game’s easy-to-understand design, and its iconic music helped Nintendo revive the home video game market in the United States.

The game also helped standardize side-scrolling platform design. Its movement, pacing, hidden secrets, and world progression became a blueprint for later platformers and for game design more broadly. It was not just popular — it became a model that many later games borrowed from.

SOUNDTRACK IMPACT

Koji Kondo’s soundtrack for Super Mario Bros. proved that game music could do more than sit in the background. The main “Ground Theme” reacts naturally to movement, energy, and rhythm, making the act of play feel musical. It helped establish the idea that game soundtracks could actively shape the player’s experience instead of just filling silence.

The Library of Congress called the theme “perhaps the most recognizable video game theme in history” and said it demonstrated that the NES sound chip was capable of musical complexity and creativity. In 2023, the “Super Mario” theme became the first video game music ever selected for the National Recording Registry, which shows how far its cultural importance extends beyond gaming.

WHY IT STILL MATTERS

Super Mario Bros. still matters because it helped define the relationship between game design and music. Its soundtrack is memorable not only because it is catchy, but because it is tightly connected to movement, tempo, and player emotion. That connection became a standard for later games across genres.

The game’s legacy shows up everywhere: platforming conventions, character branding, level design, and the expectation that a soundtrack should be part of a game’s identity. That is why Super Mario Bros. is not just historically important — it is foundational.
